🔬 Research Directions

We aim to develop novel photoenzymatic catalytic systems by integrating photochemistry, enzyme engineering, and synthetic biology. Our goal is to address challenges in traditional chemical synthesis and enable green, efficient production of high-value chiral molecules.

💡

1. Non-natural Catalysis via Direct Visible-Light Excitation of Enzymes

By directly exciting flavin-dependent enzymes (e.g., ene-reductases) with visible light, we generate reactive radical intermediates to perform non-natural asymmetric radical reactions. We have expanded beyond the natural catalytic repertoire of enzymes and developed a series of new C–C bond coupling and hydrofunctionalization reactions.

Photoenzymatic Catalysis Radical Chemistry Non-natural Reactions
📄 Representative papers: Xu, Y. et al. Nature 2024, 625, 74; Zhao, B. et al. Nat. Catal. 2023, 6, 996.
⚡

2. Electro-Enzymatic Cooperative Catalysis and Dynamic Kinetic Resolution

We combine electrochemical oxidation with enzymatic catalysis, using electrodes to replace chemical oxidants and drive dynamic kinetic resolution processes. This enables highly enantioselective synthesis of alcohols and amines, offering both green chemistry benefits and high catalytic efficiency.

Electrochemistry Dynamic Kinetic Resolution Redox
📄 Representative paper: Zhao, B. et al. Nature 2025, 643, 699.
🧬

3. Multicomponent Radical Sorting and Synergistic Catalysis

For the construction of complex molecular scaffolds, we develop synergistic systems involving photo-, enzyme, and organocatalysts to achieve ordered radical sorting and coupling of three or more components. This strategy provides new avenues for rapid generation of molecular complexity.

Multicomponent Reactions Synergistic Catalysis Radical Sorting
📄 Representative papers: Xing, Z. et al. Nature 2025, 637, 1118; Peng, X. et al. Nat. Synth. 2025, 4, 1453.
🧪

4. Rational Enzyme Engineering and Directed Evolution

Using strategies such as Focused Rational Iterative Site-specific Mutagenesis (FRISM), we reprogram natural enzymes to expand their substrate scope, enhance stability, and improve non-natural catalytic activities, thereby creating novel biocatalysts.

Enzyme Engineering Directed Evolution Rational Design
📄 Representative paper: Bao, Y. et al. Mol. Catal. 2024, 553, 113755.
